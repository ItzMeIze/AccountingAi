Section 1

The Core Concepts

Before you can prepare a classified balance sheet, you must understand the ideas that underpin it. Read through each concept carefully.

☷

A — The Accounting Equation

Assets
Everything the business owns
=
Liabilities
What the business owes to creditors
+
Owner’s Equity
The owner’s claim on assets
Key Rule: The two sides of every balance sheet must always be exactly equal. If they don’t match, there is an error somewhere in the records.

This equation shows that every dollar of assets is funded either by money the business borrowed (liabilities) or by money the owner invested or earned (equity). Nothing is unaccounted for.

📈

B — What Is “Financial Position”?

A balance sheet is formally called a Statement of Financial Position. It answers the question: “What does this business own and owe on a specific date?”

📷

It is a snapshot — like a photograph taken at a single moment in time (a specific date, e.g., “December 31, 2025”).

🕐

The income statement covers a period of time; the balance sheet covers a single day. This distinction affects the heading date format.

📋

It shows what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and what belongs to the owner (equity) on that date.

⚙

C — Simplified vs. Classified Balance Sheet

Simplified
  • Lists assets, liabilities, and equity in one block each
  • No sub-categories
  • Gives a basic overview
  • Less useful for financial decision-making
VS
Classified What We Learn
  • Splits assets into Current and Long-Term
  • Splits liabilities into Current and Long-Term
  • Shows detailed Owner’s Equity breakdown
  • Helps assess liquidity and solvency
  • Required for most real-world financial reporting
💲

D — “Claims Against Assets”

Every dollar of assets has a claim against it. There are only two types of claimants:

Total Assets
$100,000
▶
Liabilities — $40,000

Creditors are paid first. Their claim is legal.

+
Owner’s Equity — $60,000

Owner gets the residual — what’s left.

✓

E — Calculating Owner’s Equity

Assets
$120,000
−
Liabilities
$45,000
=
Owner’s Equity
$75,000

Owner’s Equity Changes Over Time

▲ Increases Equity
  • Capital investment — owner puts money/assets in
  • Net Income — revenue exceeds expenses
▼ Decreases Equity
  • Drawings — owner takes money/assets out
  • Net Loss — expenses exceed revenue
Beginning Capital  +  Investments  +  Net Income  −  Drawings  =  Ending Capital
Section 2

Balance Sheet Classifications

A classified balance sheet organises every account into one of five categories. You must know which category each account belongs to, and why.

Current Assets
CA
📅

Converted to cash or used up within one year. Listed in order of liquidity — most liquid first.

Standard Order & Why

  1. Cash — already money; the most liquid
  2. Short-Term Investments — sold in days
  3. Accounts Receivable — collected in 30–60 days
  4. Merchandise Inventory — must be sold first
  5. Prepaid Expenses — consumed, not converted
  6. Supplies — least liquid; used up gradually
Long-Term Assets
LTA
🏢

Used in operations for more than one year. Also called capital assets or fixed assets. Listed by useful life — longest-lived first.

Standard Order & Why

  1. Land — unlimited life; never depreciated
  2. Buildings — 20–40 year life
  3. Equipment — 5–15 year life
  4. Furniture & Fixtures
  5. Vehicles — 3–8 year life
Current Liabilities
CL
📅

Debts due within one year. Listed by payment urgency — most urgent first.

Standard Order & Why

  1. Bank Overdraft — immediately due
  2. Accounts Payable — owed to suppliers
  3. Wages Payable — owed to employees
  4. Interest Payable — accrued interest
  5. Unearned Revenue — service obligation
  6. Current Portion of L-T Debt
Long-Term Liabilities
LTL
🕐

Debts not due within one year. Long-term financial commitments of the business.

The portion due within 12 months must be moved to Current Liabilities and listed separately.

Common Examples

  1. Mortgage Payable
  2. Long-Term Bank Loan
  3. Bonds Payable
Owner’s Equity
OE
☰

The owner’s residual interest after all liabilities are subtracted. Represents total claim on assets.

Components on the Balance Sheet

+Capital, Beginning of PeriodWhat the owner had before this period
+Additional InvestmentsNew money/assets put into the business
+Net IncomeProfit earned during the period
or − Net Loss if expenses > revenue
−Drawings (Withdrawals)Assets taken out by owner personally
=Capital, End of PeriodOwner’s equity at end of period

What Are Drawings?

Drawings are when the owner takes cash or assets out of the business for personal use. They reduce equity and are shown parenthetically.

Note: Drawings are not an expense. They do not appear on the income statement — only on the balance sheet under Owner’s Equity.
Section 3

Formatting Rules

Accurate numbers are only half the job — marks are lost for formatting errors. Learn all 8 rules and the why behind each.

1

Three-Line Centred Heading

✓ Correct
Maple Leaf Services
Balance Sheet
As at December 31, 2025
✗ Wrong
Balance Sheet - Maple Leaf
For the Year Ended Dec. 31, 2025

Why: Line 1 = business name. Line 2 = statement name. Line 3 = single specific date. A balance sheet is a snapshot, not a period — never write “For the Year Ended.”

2

Two-Column Account Form Layout

Assets
Current Assets
Long-Term Assets

Total Assets
Liabilities & O.E.
Current Liabilities
Long-Term Liabilities
Owner’s Equity
Total L&OE

Why: Assets left, L&OE right. Both sides total to the same number — this layout makes the accounting equation visually obvious.

3

Section Sub-Headings

Under Assets write Current Assets and Long-Term Assets as bold sub-headings. Mirror this structure under Liabilities. Sub-headings are underlined and not indented.

Why: Sub-headings divide categories so readers can immediately assess liquidity — can the company pay its short-term bills from its current assets?

4

Indentation of Account Names

Current Assets
   Cash $ 14,200
   Accounts Receivable 8,500
Total Current Assets 22,700

Why: Indentation creates a visual hierarchy. Sub-headings are flush left; individual accounts are indented; totals return flush left with the amount in the far-right column.

5

Subtotals Under Each Classification

A single underline below the last item in a group signals addition. The subtotal appears below that line in the second money column.

Never skip subtotals. Markers need to verify each section independently before checking the grand total.
6

Dollar Sign Placement

Cash$ 14,200
Accounts Receivable8,500
Total Current Assets$ 22,700
Land40,000
Total Long-Term Assets$ 40,000

Why: Dollar signs appear only on the first item in each column and on each subtotal/total. Repeating $ on every line is incorrect.

7

Two Money Columns — Right-Aligned

Use an inner column for individual amounts and an outer (far-right) column for subtotals and totals. All figures must be right-aligned.

Why: Two columns immediately separate line items from totals. Right-alignment allows quick visual addition and verification.

8

Final Double Underline — The Equality Line

Total Assets$ 185,000
↔ must equal
Total Liabilities & Owner’s Equity$ 185,000

Why: A double underline signals a final, verified total. Both sides carry a double underline and must show the same number — confirming the accounting equation holds.

Section 6

Common Mistakes

These are the most frequent Grade 11 errors. Click each card to reveal the mistake and the correction.

1Listing Prepaid Expenses under Long-Term Assets
✗ Mistake

Student places Prepaid Rent under Long-Term Assets because rent is ongoing.

✓ Correction

Prepaid expenses are used up within one year — they belong in Current Assets, after Inventory.

2Placing Accounts Receivable under Liabilities
✗ Mistake

Student confuses receivable (money owed to the company) with payable (money owed by the company).

✓ Correction

Accounts Receivable is a Current Asset. The company is owed this money — that’s a resource, not an obligation.

3Forgetting to subtract Drawings from Owner’s Equity
✗ Mistake

Student lists Capital and Net Income but omits Drawings. Equity is overstated; balance sheet does not balance.

✓ Correction

Always show “Less: Drawings” and subtract. Drawings reduce equity — the owner is removing assets from the business.

4Putting the current portion of a mortgage under Long-Term Liabilities
✗ Mistake

Student lists the entire mortgage under Long-Term without splitting the current-year payment.

✓ Correction

Split: portion due within 12 months = Current Liability; remainder = Long-Term Liability. Classify by when it is due.

5Listing Current Assets in random order instead of liquidity order
✗ Mistake

Student lists Inventory first, then Cash, then Receivables. Totals may be correct but marks are lost.

✓ Correction

Always: Cash → Short-Term Investments → A/R → Inventory → Prepaid Expenses → Supplies.

6Using a date range on the heading instead of a single date
✗ Mistake

Heading reads: “For the Year Ended December 31, 2025” — copied from the income statement.

✓ Correction

Use: “As at December 31, 2025.” A balance sheet is a snapshot at one moment, not a period.

1 — The Core Concepts

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity

This is the foundation of every balance sheet. It states that everything a business owns (assets) is funded by what it owes (liabilities) plus what the owner has invested (equity). The two sides must always be equal.

What Is “Financial Position”?

A balance sheet is also called a Statement of Financial Position. It is a snapshot — taken on a single date — showing what a company owns, what it owes, and what belongs to the owner. Think of it like a photograph of the company’s finances at midnight on that date.

Simplified vs. Classified Balance Sheet

Simplified

  • Lists assets, liabilities, and equity in one block each
  • No sub-categories
  • Quick overview, less detail

Classified What We’re Learning

  • Breaks assets into Current and Long-Term
  • Breaks liabilities into Current and Long-Term
  • Shows Owner’s Equity detail (capital, drawings)
  • More useful for decision-making

What Are “Claims Against Assets”?

Every asset the company holds has someone with a claim on it:

  • Creditors (people/companies the business owes money to) have the first claim — these are Liabilities.
  • The owner has a residual claim on whatever is left — this is Owner’s Equity.

Together, liabilities + owner’s equity account for all claims on every dollar of assets.

Calculating Owner’s Equity

Owner’s Equity = Assets − Liabilities

Example: If a company owns $120,000 in assets and owes $45,000, then Owner’s Equity = $120,000 − $45,000 = $75,000.

Owner’s equity increases with investments and net income, and decreases with withdrawals (drawings) and net losses.

2 — Balance Sheet Classifications

CA

Current Assets

Items that will be converted to cash or used up within one year (or one operating cycle). Listed in order of liquidity — the most liquid (closest to cash) first.

Typical Order:

  1. Cash — already liquid
  2. Short-Term Investments — easily sold
  3. Accounts Receivable — money owed by customers
  4. Merchandise Inventory — goods to be sold
  5. Prepaid Expenses — rent, insurance paid in advance
  6. Supplies — office/store supplies on hand
LTA

Long-Term Assets (Capital / Fixed)

Assets used in business operations for more than one year. They are not intended for resale. Listed roughly by useful life (longest-lived first).

Typical Order:

  1. Land — unlimited life, not depreciated
  2. Building — long useful life
  3. Equipment — moderate useful life
  4. Furniture & Fixtures
  5. Vehicles — shorter useful life
CL

Current Liabilities

Debts that must be paid within one year. Listed in the order they are typically paid first.

Typical Order:

  1. Bank Overdraft / Line of Credit
  2. Accounts Payable — owed to suppliers
  3. Wages Payable — owed to employees
  4. Interest Payable
  5. Unearned Revenue — payment received before service
  6. Current Portion of Long-Term Debt
LTL

Long-Term Liabilities

Debts that are not due within one year. They extend beyond the current operating period.

Examples:

  1. Mortgage Payable — loan secured by property
  2. Long-Term Bank Loan
  3. Bonds Payable
OE

Owner’s Equity

The owner’s residual interest in the business after all liabilities are subtracted from assets.

Components

  • Capital (beginning) — what the owner had at the start of the period
  • + Additional Investments — new money or assets the owner puts in
  • + Net Income (or − Net Loss) — profit earned during the period
  • − Withdrawals (Drawings) — money/assets the owner takes out for personal use

Ending Capital Formula

Beginning Capital + Investments + Net Income − Drawings = Ending Capital

3 — Formatting Rules

A balance sheet must follow strict formatting conventions. Here are the rules you need to know:

1

Three-Line Heading

Every balance sheet starts with:
Line 1: Company Name
Line 2: “Balance Sheet” (or “Classified Balance Sheet”)
Line 3: The specific date (e.g., “As at December 31, 2025”)

2

Two-Column (Report or Account Form) Layout

Left side: Assets
Right side: Liabilities and Owner’s Equity
Both sides must reach the same total on the bottom line.

3

Section Sub-Headings

Under Assets, write “Current Assets” and “Long-Term Assets” as sub-headings. Same approach for Liabilities.

4

Indentation

Individual account names are indented under their sub-heading. This creates a clear visual hierarchy.

5

Subtotals & Totals

A single underline under the last item in a section signals addition. The subtotal appears below. Double underline marks the final total on each side.

6

Dollar Alignment

All dollar amounts in a column must be right-aligned. Dollar signs appear only on the first amount in each column and on the final total.

7

Equality Line

The very bottom of the balance sheet must show:
Total Assets = Total Liabilities and Owner’s Equity
Both figures should be identical and double-underlined.

8

Spacing & Neatness

Leave consistent vertical spacing between sections. Never crowd numbers. Use blank rows between Current and Long-Term groups for readability.

6 — Common Mistakes

Click each card to reveal the mistake and learn the correction.

1 Listing Prepaid Rent under Long-Term Assets

Why it’s wrong: Prepaid Rent is used up within one year, so it is a Current Asset.

Fix: Move it under Current Assets, after Inventory (since it’s less liquid than receivables or inventory).

2 Placing Accounts Receivable under Liabilities

Why it’s wrong: Accounts Receivable is money owed to the business — it is an asset, not a liability.

Fix: List it as a Current Asset, right after Cash.

3 Forgetting to subtract Drawings from Owner’s Equity

Why it’s wrong: Drawings reduce the owner’s claim. If you skip them, equity is overstated and the sheet won’t balance.

Fix: Always show “Less: Drawings” and subtract from capital.

4 Putting the Current Portion of a Mortgage under Long-Term Liabilities

Why it’s wrong: The portion due within 12 months is a Current Liability, even though the mortgage is long-term overall.

Fix: Split the mortgage: current portion under Current Liabilities, remainder under Long-Term Liabilities.

5 Listing assets in random order instead of by liquidity

Why it’s wrong: Current assets must be ordered from most liquid to least liquid. This convention helps readers quickly assess the company’s ability to pay short-term debts.

Fix: Cash → Short-Term Investments → A/R → Inventory → Prepaid Expenses → Supplies.

6 Using a date range instead of a single date

Why it’s wrong: A balance sheet is a snapshot of one moment. A date range (e.g., “For the Year Ended…”) belongs on an income statement.

Fix: Use “As at [single date]” — e.g., “As at December 31, 2025.”
